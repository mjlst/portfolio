identitysmart.com
Product revamp

Previously the user flow of IdentitySmart had several recurring loops without any indication on progress. Also some of the sections and pages were copied from previous product that wasn't created for the current users.

Focused Area

Previously, identitysmart.com was part of a the other product, peoplesmart.com. As business grows, the team decided to separate the identitysmart.com and to provide users in-depth identity monitoring service. Even though the webpage for identity smart was there, the branding, the user flow and the member's area dashboard were all part of the PeopleSmart product which confused a number of users. To begin with, I was focusing on below three key sections. This included developing new branding and style guide, conducting usability testing, and providing high fidelity mockup to the engineers.

1Checkout Flow

Checkout flow required the system to not only collect the payment information, but also to increase the activation rate.

2Member Area Dashboard

Users should be able to directly access to alerts from the dashboard. Also menu items and contents must be reorganized.

3Landing Page

identitysmart.com landing page is to inform the visitors about the service and explain the value proposition.

How to help users understand Identity Monitoring Service?

Non-Member Area Landing Page

Our goal for designing the landing page was to explain the importance of purchasing identity monitoring service and how they can actively utilize the service to protect their identity. Previously, identitysmart.com was explaining the concept with very generic terms that audience might not understand. Therefore, it was important for the design team to closely working with the contents team. We went through a number of iterations including the changes in contents as well as the design. Since the contents were strongly tied up with the structure of the page, often contents team and design team were sitting side-by-side to go through details iterations. This process helped us to streamline the process of designing the landing page.

Also, IdentitySmart team was planning to actively involved in business solutions where preparing business owners to help their customers from any types of identity theft. To specifically target the business owners, we also created a separate page for them so that they can receive information that can match their interests.

Two versions of Checkout Flow

Previously, IdentitySmart was a section of a product called PeopleSmart and it was an additional product that was provided to PeopleSmart users. Because of that, there was no specific payment flow in the product. Also, it required different sets of information than the previous product.

1Combined activation and checkout flow

It is important for the service coverage to be started as soon as the user submits the payment method. Therefore the first design was collecting personal information before the payment method was submitted. This flow was helpful to increase the activation rate. However, this was overburden the users asking for a number of information at once.

Suggested Interaction

For the combined checkout flow, I came up with a set of form interaction to achieve below objectives.

1)Users can see the types of information they are going to fill in at a glance.

2)Form is grouped in a chunk of related information.

3)Users can see the label of the input field, even after they type in the information.

4)Users can see in which stage of the process they are in including section that they are currently at, input of invalid value, and successful submission.

This prototype is created using HTML/CSS and Javascript.
* Individual input and label interaction was referenced from google material design.

2Separated activation and checkout flow

In the second design, activation flow and payment flow were separated. First users creates account, then submits the payment method. After this, users can access activation flow to start the monitoring service. In this flow, it is important to prompt users to finish the activation so that the full service coverage can start.

Based on the A/B testing conducted, we decided to deliver the design with the separate activation flow. Here, the activation rate was doubled when users were provided with small sections of inputs. In this case, it was important to clearly indicate the progress of the activation flow.

Enabling Prompt Reaction on Identity Theft Alerts

Member Area Dashboard

When a customer first signs up to the service and completes the activation, the dashboard is the first page that they encounter. Previously, in the dashboard, different types of alerts were separated into categories and stored in different pages to be viewed. However, we learned that users were not aware of the differences of those categories and they were more concerned about whether the alert was related to any of financial losses. To help users to resolve any issues without them looking for the alerts, we revamped the member area where they can see all the recent alerts and credit scores, as well as they can react to them. We also kept the categories as part of the alerts after learning that users determine the importance of alerts based on the types. For example, they cared more about alerts related to SSN and tried to resolve the issue as soon as possible.

Design Goals for Dashboard Redesign

1)Alerts must be accessible from the dashboard area.

2)Possible options to react to suspected fraud alerts should be available.

3)Menus and pages must be reorganized.

4)Supporting information should be provided actively.

Dashboard Prototyping and Usability Testing

To design alert section's interaction that allows users to quickly respond to the alerts, I created a prototype page with a few different approaches for the design and conducted 5 internal usability testing. I used bootstrap framework for this prototype. The list below shows the iterations tested and observations made from the tests.

1)Tab implementation for archived alerts — This helped testers to find previously archived alerts.

2)Notifying users when alerts archived — Testers were ignoring most of archived alert notification.

3)Providing category of the alerts — Testers paid more attention to SSN related alerts than Email breaches.

4)Organizing the alerts from the latest to the oldest — Testers valued more when alerts were organized by risk rather than by time.
